98% of employees are already using unsanctioned AI tools. For EU CISOs, that statistic is not an abstract risk — it's a NIS2 incident waiting to happen.

The directive covers 160,000+ EU entities and carries fines up to €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover. Yet most security teams are still evaluating AI risk through a traditional DLP lens. That's a structural mismatch. Traditional DLP was built for file transfers and email exfiltration — not conversational AI traffic, not prompt injection, not real-time data masking before a query reaches an LLM.

What NIS2-aligned AI security actually requires:

— Active incident prevention, not post-breach logging. When 8.5% of prompts contain sensitive data and AI-specific attacks are rising 67% year-on-year, detection latency is measured in damage, not milliseconds.

— Audit-ready evidence. Regulators want traceable decision trails, not dashboard screenshots. Every detection event needs a human-readable record that survives an enforcement review.

— Governance that covers shadow AI. 75% of organisations using shadow AI have no governance layer over it. That exposure sits squarely within NIS2's incident reporting scope.

— EU AI Act alignment in the same platform. Article 10 (data governance), Article 14 (human oversight with operator override), Article 15 (robustness against AI-specific threats) — these aren't separate workstreams. They belong in a single, auditable control layer.

The compliance cost pressure is real: 40% of EU IT spending already goes toward compliance-related costs. The architecture decision you make now determines whether AI governance adds to that burden or consolidates it.

For security architects currently designing AI governance frameworks: where is the biggest gap in your current stack — incident detection, audit trails, or policy enforcement across multiple LLM providers?
