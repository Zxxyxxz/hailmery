The threat categories driving breach costs in 2026

Prompt injection remains the #1 OWASP AI security risk. It is not a novel edge case — it is the dominant attack pattern against enterprise LLM deployments. Alongside it, jailbreaking, data exfiltration, social engineering, and model inversion make up the five core AI threat categories that security controls must address by design, not as an afterthought.

Beyond these five, eight advanced threat categories — including adversarial attacks, IP theft, business logic attacks, and context attacks — are documented and actively observed. Coordinated multi-vector campaigns are especially difficult to detect: when an attacker combines prompt injection with encoding obfuscation, a single-category detector will miss it. Defenses need composite scoring across all active vectors simultaneously.

Encoding obfuscation alone covers more than 26 documented attack vectors — from Base64 and Unicode manipulation to null byte injection and bidirectional text override — running up to three levels of nested encoding recursion to evade inspection.

Shadow AI is not a user behaviour problem — it is a structural control gap

98% of employees use unsanctioned AI applications. 75% of organisations running shadow AI have no governance policies in place. 8.5% of all prompts submitted to AI tools contain sensitive information — PII, credentials, or internal data — and that figure applies equally to unsanctioned tools as to approved ones.

The breach cost differential is quantifiable: organisations using shadow AI see breach costs $670,000 higher than those without. Traditional DLP was designed for file movement and email. It does not inspect prompt content, does not operate inline within an LLM request pipeline, and does not enforce policy at the point of AI model interaction. That is a distinct control requirement from what legacy DLP addresses.

NIS2 and the EU AI Act: what the enforcement timeline means for your controls

NIS2 applies to 160,000+ entities across the EU. Non-compliance carries fines up to €10 million or 2% of global turnover. The directive requires demonstrable incident prevention and audit-ready reporting — neither of which is satisfied by post-hoc logging alone.

The EU AI Act introduces additional obligations specifically for high-risk AI systems: data governance controls (Article 10), human oversight mechanisms (Article 14), and cybersecurity robustness (Article 15). 68% of European businesses report difficulty understanding their EU AI Act responsibilities. The requirement for traceable, human-readable audit trails of every AI-driven decision is not optional for high-risk deployments — it is a compliance condition.

What adequate AI security controls look like in 2026

Adequate controls operate inline — inspecting every request through a structured security pipeline before it reaches an AI provider, and inspecting every response before it reaches the user. They cover content safety, semantic threat detection across 27+ threat types, pattern-based data leakage prevention with 950+ pre-configured rules for PII, PHI, PCI, credentials, and API keys, and they enforce policy at sub-millisecond latency without requiring code changes to existing AI integrations.

Controls that only log events after the fact do not constitute incident prevention under NIS2. Controls that rely on single-category detection will miss coordinated multi-vector attacks. And controls that require months of implementation work will not close the gap fast enough — 97% of AI-related breaches involved systems lacking proper access controls, which means the exposure is not theoretical.
